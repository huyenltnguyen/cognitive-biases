You can explain what a cognitive bias is as a predictable output of a fast-thinking brain in situations designed to trigger its shortcuts.

You can recognize four patterns:

- Filtering information to confirm what you already believe
- Judging probability by how vivid examples feel
- Holding on because losses hurt and past investments bind
- Going along with groups even when you privately disagree

You have four tools to reach for before consequential decisions:

- Look for the strongest case against your current view
- Ask what usually happens in situations like this before reasoning from the specifics of yours
- Ask whether you would start this today if you had no prior investment
- Write down your position before hearing what others think

None of this makes you immune. The patterns don't stop running just because you know their names. But knowing what to look for, and having a concrete step to take when you notice it, is a solid place to start.
