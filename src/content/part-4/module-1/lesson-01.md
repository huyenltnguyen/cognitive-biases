By 1983, Coca-Cola's share of the cola market had fallen to under 24 percent, down from 60 percent in the years after World War II. Pepsi had closed the gap steadily, partly through a high-profile marketing campaign — the Pepsi Challenge — in which shoppers at malls and supermarkets were handed two unmarked cups and asked which they preferred. In blind sip tests, most chose Pepsi. PepsiCo ran the results as national ads.

Inside Coca-Cola, the results created pressure. Roberto Goizueta, who had become CEO in 1980, commissioned a secret project, internally named "Project Kansas", to develop a new formula. The goal was a Coke that would beat Pepsi in blind taste tests. The new formula was sweeter and smoother. In testing, it worked: the new formula outperformed both original Coke and Pepsi in sip tests run across nearly 200,000 participants.

The research team presented the results. The numbers were clear and consistent. There was some contrary signal in the data, about 10 to 12 percent of participants expressed strong negative reactions, including anger at the idea of any change, and focus groups showed some participants upset at the thought that the original formula would be discontinued. But those reactions were noted and then set aside. The majority preferred the new taste. Surveys, given more weight than focus groups in the marketing practices of the era, showed broadly positive results.

The team's conclusion was confident: the new formula was better, and the data proved it.
