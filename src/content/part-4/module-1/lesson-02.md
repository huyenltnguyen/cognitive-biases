Goizueta and the executive team made the decision: they would replace the original formula entirely. The original Coke would be discontinued. On April 23, 1985, the new formula launched nationally under the name New Coke.

The backlash was immediate and unlike anything the company had anticipated. The Coca-Cola hotline, which normally received around 400 calls a day, began receiving over 1,500 calls a day, almost all of them complaints. The company received more than 40,000 calls and letters expressing anger or disappointment, including one addressed to "Chief Dodo, the Coca-Cola Company." Protest groups formed. In Atlanta, where Coca-Cola was founded, local sentiment was especially fierce.

Seventy-nine days after the launch, on July 11, 1985, Coca-Cola held a press conference and announced the return of the original formula under the name Coca-Cola Classic. The announcement interrupted regular television programming. A senator called the reintroduction "a meaningful moment in U.S. history." The hotline received over 31,000 calls in the two days that followed — nearly all of them expressions of relief and gratitude.

After the fact, the company spent considerable time reviewing what had gone wrong. Their eventual conclusion was that the research had answered the question it was asked — which formula tasted better in a sip test — and had failed to ask what the product meant to the people who bought it. As Coca-Cola president Donald Keough said at the press conference: "The simple fact is that all the time and money and skill poured into consumer research on the new Coca-Cola could not measure or reveal the deep and abiding emotional attachment to original Coca-Cola felt by so many people."

The taste tests were real, the methods were standard, and the new formula really did come out ahead. But the jump from that result to the belief that people would accept the change was not backed by the data. It was an assumption.
