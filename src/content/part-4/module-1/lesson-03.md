exercise:
  kind: multiple-choice
  mode: multi
  question: 'Select the three patterns most clearly present in the New Coke decision-making process.'
  options:
    - id: confirmation
      label: 'Seeing What You Expect (confirmation bias / anchoring)'
      explanation: 'Present — and central. The research process was built around a question the team already had an answer to: which formula wins a blind taste test? Results that confirmed the case for change were weighted heavily. The 10–12 percent of participants who reacted with anger, and the focus groups that showed emotional attachment to the original, produced a signal that was noted and set aside rather than investigated.'
    - id: availability
      label: "Misjudging What's Likely (availability / base rate neglect)"
      explanation: 'Present. The Pepsi Challenge data was vivid, concrete, and repeated — mall shoppers choosing Pepsi on camera became the dominant image of where the market was heading. That vivid data crowded out a harder-to-picture factor: what a long-standing product means to people who have been buying it for decades, which does not show up in a sip test.'
    - id: sunk-cost
      label: 'Holding On When You Should Let Go (loss aversion / sunk cost / framing)'
      explanation: 'Not a central factor here. The decision was to change, not to hold on. The patterns more directly at work were in how evidence was read and which signals were amplified.'
    - id: groupthink
      label: 'Going Along with the Group (conformity / groupthink / FAE)'
      explanation: "Present. The research team's conclusion was confident, and the numbers were consistent. In that environment, the 10–12 percent who reacted with anger and the focus groups that showed distress were treated as minor noise rather than a signal worth investigating. The internal momentum toward the new formula made it harder for contrary views to surface or be taken seriously."
  correct_option_ids:
    - confirmation
    - availability
    - groupthink
