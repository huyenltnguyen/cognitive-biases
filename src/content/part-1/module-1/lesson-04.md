To make fast thinking work, your brain relies on mental shortcuts, rules of thumb that produce good-enough answers quickly. These are called heuristics. They are efficient, usually accurate, and essential for a world that moves faster than careful deliberation allows.

A heuristic works like a compressed judgment: instead of reasoning from scratch, your brain substitutes a question it can answer quickly. "Is this person trustworthy?" becomes "Does this person remind me of trustworthy people I've known?" "How common is this problem?" becomes "How easily can I think of an example?"

The substitution usually works. But it fails in specific types of situations: when the pattern you're matching to is misleading, when the example you recall is unrepresentative, or when the first number you encountered was arbitrary. In these situations, the shortcut produces the same directional error reliably across people and cultures.

That predictability is what makes cognitive biases worth studying. Random errors can't be anticipated. Consistent, directional errors can — which means you can learn to recognize the situations where the substitution tends to go wrong, and pause.
