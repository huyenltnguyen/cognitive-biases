Most of your daily decisions happen automatically — you don't deliberate about them. You recognize a colleague's voice in another room. You know without thinking that a green light means go. You finish a familiar phrase before someone finishes saying it. These recognitions happen in fractions of a second, without effort, and almost always correctly.

But some decisions require a different mode. Choosing which apartment to rent. Working out whether two contracts offer the same terms. Understanding why a project is running behind when you've never encountered this particular problem before. These require deliberate, sequential effort. You have to slow down, gather information, and reason step by step.

Psychologists call these two modes fast and slow thinking. Both are essential. Fast thinking handles the thousands of small decisions that would be overwhelming to deliberate about. Slow thinking handles complexity that fast thinking can't resolve.

The problem is that fast thinking doesn't always know when to hand off. It makes quick calls in situations that deserve slow deliberation. And it does this confidently, with no sense that anything unusual happened.

Cognitive biases live at this handoff point. They happen when fast thinking produces a quick answer to a question that needed slow thinking, and we don't notice the substitution.
