This course covers four patterns of bias, each a different way the fast-thinking system produces predictable errors.

**Pattern 1: Seeing What You Expect.** We don't search for the truth; we search for confirmation of what we already believe. Evidence that fits our existing view gets noticed and remembered. Evidence that challenges it gets discounted or explained away.

**Pattern 2: Misjudging What's Likely.** We judge how probable something is by how easily we can think of an example. Events we've recently heard about, or that are vivid and dramatic, feel far more common than they are. Quiet, routine events feel rarer than they are.

**Pattern 3: Holding On When You Should Let Go.** Losses tend to feel more painful than equivalent gains feel good — research suggests the asymmetry may be roughly 2:1, though the magnitude varies by context. This asymmetry makes us hold onto things that aren't working, continue investing in failures, and make different decisions depending on how options are framed, even when the underlying facts are identical.

**Pattern 4: Going Along with the Group.** In groups, social pressure suppresses independent thinking. We adjust our views toward what others seem to believe. And we routinely apply different standards to the same behavior depending on who is doing it.

Each of the four modules in Part 2 introduces one of these patterns. No prior knowledge of psychology is required, just willingness to observe your own thinking as you go.
