We estimate the probability of an event by how easily we can bring examples to mind. If an example comes to mind quickly and vividly, the event feels common. If examples are hard to retrieve, the event feels rare.

This substitution works reasonably well when personal experience is representative of reality. But it fails systematically when easily retrievable examples are unrepresentative in one of four specific ways:

- **Dramatic or disturbing events** get covered in the news, discussed in conversation, and encoded in memory with high detail. They are easy to recall — but they are often rare.
- **Routine, unremarkable events** leave little memory trace. They are hard to recall — but they may be very common.
- **Personally experienced events** feel vivid and significant, even when they were exceptional rather than typical.
- **Recently encountered information** is more accessible than older information, making recent events feel more probable.

The table in Lesson 1 showed this at scale. The events people most significantly overestimated are dramatic and newsworthy. The ones most underestimated are widespread but slow and unremarkable.
