When we make predictions about a specific situation, we focus on its unique details and underweight the statistical base rate — what typically happens in similar situations.

Base rates summarize what actually happened across many real cases: the percentage of projects that run over budget, the typical time to complete a certain type of application, the fraction of new ventures that succeed in their first year. They are usually the most accurate single predictor of what will happen next in a similar situation.

But they don't feel informative. When you're planning your specific project, with your specific team and circumstances, the general statistic seems to belong to someone else's situation. "Those other cases were different" is a natural and almost universal reaction.

That reaction is usually wrong. The current situation is almost always more similar to the reference class than it feels. This tendency to treat the current case as exceptional — to reason from the inside rather than from the base rate — produces systematic optimism about timelines, costs, and outcomes.
