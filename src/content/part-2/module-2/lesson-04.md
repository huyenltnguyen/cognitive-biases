The availability heuristic shapes ordinary judgments in ways that feel entirely like evidence-based reasoning.

After hearing about a flood in the news, people in unaffected areas buy more flood insurance — then let it lapse after a few years, once the memory fades. What changed was not the risk. What changed was how easily the scenario came to mind.

A hiring panel that recently made a poor hire will raise the bar across the next several candidates — calibrating against a vivid exception rather than against the full picture of their hiring outcomes. Candidates who would have been rated favorably a year ago are now seen as not quite good enough.

A person who has never known anyone injured in a cycling accident may significantly underestimate the risk of cycling without a helmet because the scenario simply does not come to mind.

In each case, the estimate feels accurate. It doesn't announce itself as a shortcut. It shows up as a considered assessment, arrived at through experience and observation.
