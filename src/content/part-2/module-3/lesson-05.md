People respond to the same information differently depending on how it's presented. Options described as gains produce more cautious, risk-averse choices. Options described as losses produce more risk-seeking choices. People will accept a gamble to avoid a certain loss, even when the gamble is objectively worse in expected value.

This is a direct consequence of loss aversion. Because losses feel worse than gains feel good, we're motivated to avoid certain losses even at the cost of worse expected outcomes overall.

The practical implication: the person who controls how options are presented in a discussion influences the outcome through framing alone. Two people presenting the same data can steer a group toward different decisions simply by choosing which framing to lead with.

This rarely happens deliberately. It often occurs without the presenter realizing it. It also occurs without the group realizing that framing, rather than substance, has influenced their judgment.
