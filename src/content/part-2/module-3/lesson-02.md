In Lesson 1, both pairs had a forward-looking option that the evidence supported, and an option that felt harder to walk away from. In Pair 1, shelving the feature meant treating 8 months of work as gone. In Pair 2, switching tools meant giving up 5 years of familiarity. If you leaned toward shipping or staying, that pull had a name: loss aversion.

Loss aversion is the tendency to weight potential losses more heavily than equivalent gains. A 2024 meta-analysis of 607 empirical estimates across 150 studies found that losses weigh approximately twice as heavily as equivalent gains on average (Brown et al., *Journal of Economic Literature*). What you stand to lose feels more urgent than what you stand to gain, even when the rational case points the other way.

In the pairs from Lesson 1, the 8 months and the 5 years were sunk — already spent, not recoverable either way. The better decision was the one that made sense going forward. But loss aversion reframes sunk costs as something you can still protect, making it harder to let go even when letting go is clearly right.
