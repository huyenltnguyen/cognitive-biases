We are, in general, bad at walking away from things we've invested in. When we've put time, money, or effort into something, stopping feels like a loss — even when the rational analysis says to stop. This is the sunk cost fallacy: letting past, unrecoverable investment drive future decisions.

Everyday life examples:

- Finishing a film you don't enjoy because you're already 45 minutes in.
- Continuing to attend a course that isn't helping because you already paid the fee.
- Persisting with a plan that isn't working because of the hours already invested.
- Remaining in a relationship that does not make you happy, solely because of the "years you've spent together".

In each case, the investment is already gone regardless of what you decide. The only relevant question is: "Going forward, is this worth continuing?" The sunk cost is not part of that calculation, yet it reliably feels like it should be.

Not all continuation reasoning is sunk cost. Staying the course on a long-term goal despite short-term setbacks can be strategically sound. The fallacy is specifically when past investment is the main reason to continue, despite evidence that future value is negative.
