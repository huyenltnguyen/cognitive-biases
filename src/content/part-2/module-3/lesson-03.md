Look back at your answers from Lesson 1.

In Pair 1, the evidence said customers don't want the feature. The rational move was to shelve it. But 8 months of work already spent made that feel like a loss — and if you chose to ship, loss aversion was part of the reason.
In Pair 1, the evidence said customers don't want the feature. The rational move was to shelve it. But 8 months of work already spent made that feel like a loss, and if you chose to ship, loss aversion was part of the reason.

In Pair 2, the newer tool was cheaper and better rated. The rational move was to switch. But 5 years of familiarity made abandoning the old tool feel like giving something up; if you chose to stay, that feeling likely had more influence than you noticed.

In both cases, the past investment was already gone either way. It could not be recovered by shipping or by staying. What loss aversion does is make sunk costs feel like something still at stake, something you can protect by holding on. That feeling is real. The logic behind it is not.
