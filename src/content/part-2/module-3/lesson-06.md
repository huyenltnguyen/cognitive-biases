  - kind: multiple-choice
    mode: single
    question: 'Scenario 1 — A public health department is asking a city council to fund a new vaccination program.'
    options:
      - id: frame1-1
        label: 'Frame 1: This vaccine prevents serious illness in 6 out of 10 people who receive it.'
        explanation: 'Correct. Frame 1 presents the program as a gain — protection is emphasized. Gain framing generates more favorable responses to the same data. The same statistic described as a failure rate in Frame 2 triggers more resistance, even though the underlying data is identical.'
      - id: frame2-1
        label: 'Frame 2: 4 in 10 people who receive this vaccine still develop serious illness.'
        explanation: 'Frame 2 highlights cases where the vaccine does not work — a loss frame. Loss framing makes the same program appear less effective and generates more resistance, even though nothing about the vaccine has changed.'
      - id: same-1
        label: 'Both frames would produce the same response — the statistics are equivalent.'
        explanation: 'Research consistently shows that framing effects persist even when people recognize that the data is identical. The way information is presented influences judgment independently of its content.'
    correct_option_ids:
      - frame1-1
  - kind: multiple-choice
    mode: single
    question: 'Scenario 2 — A school district is asking its board to fund a new after-school tutoring program.'
    options:
      - id: frame1-2
        label: 'Frame 1: 3 out of 5 students who complete this program improve their grade by at least one level.'
        explanation: 'Correct. Frame 1 emphasizes improvement — a gain frame. Gain framing generates more favorable responses to the same data. The same statistic expressed as a failure rate in Frame 2 makes the identical program appear less effective, even though nothing about the program has changed.'
      - id: frame2-2
        label: 'Frame 2: 2 in 5 students who complete this program do not show a grade improvement.'
        explanation: 'Frame 2 highlights the students the program does not help — a loss frame. Loss framing makes the same program appear less effective and generates more resistance, even though the underlying data is identical.'
      - id: same-2
        label: 'Both frames would produce the same response — the outcome data is equivalent.'
        explanation: 'Research consistently shows that framing effects persist even when people recognize that the data is identical. The way information is presented influences judgment independently of its content.'
    correct_option_ids:
      - frame1-2
  - kind: multiple-choice
    mode: single
    question: 'Scenario 3 — A finance team is presenting a cost-reduction plan to company leadership for approval.'
    options:
      - id: frame1-3
        label: "Frame 1: Under this plan, 12 of the company's 20 active projects will continue running."
        explanation: 'Correct. Frame 1 emphasizes what is preserved — a gain frame. Describing the plan in terms of continuity generates more favorable responses than describing it in terms of cuts, even though both frames represent the same decision.'
      - id: frame2-3
        label: "Frame 2: Under this plan, 8 of the company's 20 active projects will be discontinued."
        explanation: 'Frame 2 draws attention to what is lost — a loss frame. Describing the outcome as discontinuing projects makes the decision feel more costly and generates more resistance, even though the underlying plan is identical.'
      - id: same-3
        label: 'Both frames would produce the same response — leadership will focus on the substance of the plan.'
        explanation: 'Framing effects operate even among experienced decision-makers. Research shows that executives and boards respond differently to gain-framed versus loss-framed presentations of identical proposals.'
    correct_option_ids:
      - frame1-3
