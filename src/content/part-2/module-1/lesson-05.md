When we make estimates or judgments, the first piece of information we encounter tends to shape everything that follows. A number, a description, a comparison, or a stated constraint can all serve as a starting point. We adjust from there, but research suggests the adjustment is almost always insufficient. The initial value pulls the final judgment toward itself even when we later recognize it was arbitrary or irrelevant.

Englich and Mussweiler demonstrated this in a 2001 study using actual trial judges as participants. Each judge reviewed the same case materials for an alleged rape and was told either that the prosecutor was demanding a 2-month sentence or a 34-month sentence. The sentencing demand is a routine part of a real trial — yet it is the prosecutor's opening position, not evidence. Judges who received the high anchor handed down sentences averaging nearly 10 months longer than those who received the low anchor, on identical evidence. In a follow-up study, the researchers replaced the prosecutor's demand with the outcome of a dice roll shown to the judge before deliberation. The effect held.

Later research extended the finding to professional contexts. In a 1987 study by Northcraft and Neale, both students and professional real estate agents toured an actual property and received a detailed information packet. The only variable between groups was the listing price they were shown. The listing price significantly influenced all four measures researchers tracked — appraised value, recommended selling price, a reasonable purchase price, and the lowest offer they would accept. Expertise offered no protection: the effect held for agents regardless of experience. What made the finding especially striking was this: when asked afterwards which factors had influenced their estimates, only 19% of expert agents mentioned the listing price. The anchor had shaped their judgments without their awareness.

The anchor doesn't have to be a number. A project described as "almost done" shapes timeline expectations differently than one described as "in progress." A candidate introduced as "overqualified" frames how an interview unfolds before a word is spoken. A budget figure mentioned in passing at the start of a meeting sets a ceiling for what feels achievable for the rest of it. In each case, the initial piece of information does more work than the evidence that follows — and neither the person who introduced it nor the people responding to it tend to notice.
