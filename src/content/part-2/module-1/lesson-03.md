Confirmation bias is the tendency to search for, notice, interpret, and remember information in ways that confirm what you already believe. It operates across the entire reasoning process:

- **What you look for:** You seek out sources and perspectives that are likely to agree with you.
- **What you notice:** Evidence that fits your view is easy to spot; evidence against it blends into the background.
- **How you interpret ambiguous information:** When a piece of evidence could support either view, it tends to get interpreted as supporting yours.
- **What you remember:** Confirming evidence is retained; challenging evidence is forgotten or recalled as less significant than it was.

---

Look at how each layer played out in the opening scenario:

- **What she looked for:** She consulted the colleagues she knew best — who happened to share her existing positive view of the vendor. She did not seek out anyone with a different experience.
- **What she noticed:** The billing complaint had documentation, so it got a thorough investigation. The food quality complaints were vague, so they were easier to set aside.
- **How she interpreted ambiguous information:** Two complaints in three years could signal an emerging problem or be genuinely rare. She interpreted them as rare.
- **What she remembered and reported:** The billing investigation produced a clear finding that favored the vendor. The food complaints were summarized in a single phrase — "isolated incidents" — and carried little weight in her recommendation.

The reviewer was not deliberately misleading anyone. Each decision seemed reasonable on its own. This is how confirmation bias usually works — it operates below awareness, and from the inside, it feels like careful evaluation.
