Groupthink describes what happens when a group under pressure toward consensus stops genuinely testing its conclusions. Members self-censor concerns to preserve group harmony. Dissent gets interpreted as disloyalty or as not being "on board." The group develops a sense of false unanimity — a belief that everyone agrees, when in fact people simply didn't raise what they privately thought.

Warning signs:

- Decisions reached without anyone articulating a contrary view
- Concerns raised and quickly moved past, without real engagement
- People agreeing in the meeting and raising doubts afterward in the hallway
- A strong or senior voice early in the discussion that no one challenges

It is important to distinguish groupthink from genuine consensus. A group that discusses a decision thoroughly, surfaces disagreement, and still reaches a shared conclusion has not experienced groupthink. The test is whether the process allowed genuine challenge — not whether everyone ended up in the same place.

Note: groupthink is a widely used practical lens; its empirical validation as a precise causal model is limited. It is most useful as a set of warning signs to recognize in group dynamics.
