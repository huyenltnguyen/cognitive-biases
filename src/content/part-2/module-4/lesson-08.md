In the previous exercise, most people select explanations that point to the colleague's character — they're disorganized, they don't prioritize, they didn't flag the problem. Now consider: if you missed the same deadline, which explanation would you give? Most likely a situational one — something came up, the scope turned out to be larger than expected, another commitment interfered.

This asymmetry is called the fundamental attribution error: when other people do something, we explain it by who they are. When we do the same thing, we explain it by what was happening around us.

The reason is straightforward. You have direct access to your own circumstances — you know what else was on your plate, what went wrong, what you were dealing with. For other people, you don't have that context. All you see is the behavior, so you fill in the gap with a character explanation: they're careless, they don't care, they're not competent.

This is not a moral failing. It is a gap in available information, combined with a tendency to treat observable behavior as more revealing than unobservable context. The character explanation may not be the final judgment — but it is often the first one that surfaces, and it shapes the questions we ask and the assumptions we carry into the conversation.

The error is called "fundamental" because it underlies a wide range of judgment patterns — how we evaluate colleagues, how we interpret mistakes, how we assign blame when things go wrong. Recognizing the pattern does not remove it, but it can prompt you to ask about someone's circumstances before settling on a character explanation.
