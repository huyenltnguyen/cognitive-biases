The chair acknowledges it and says the team will monitor it post-decision. No one else speaks up. The decision is reached in 15 minutes. Afterward, the dissenting member tells a colleague: 'I had more to say, but it didn't feel like the moment.'"
attempts_allowed: -1
exercise:
  kind: multiple-choice
  mode: single
  question: 'Which behavior most clearly signals groupthink?'
  options:
    - id: fast-decision
      label: The decision was reached quickly.
      explanation: "Speed alone isn't a signal of groupthink — a genuinely straightforward decision can be reached quickly. The key signal is whether dissent was possible and surfaced, not how long the meeting took."
    - id: private-concerns
      label: The dissenting member had more to say but didn't raise it in the meeting.
      explanation: 'Correct. Concerns held back during the meeting, while agreement is performed publicly, are the defining signal of groupthink. The meeting record shows consensus, but the actual state of agreement was not. Compare this with a version where members ask specific questions, request data before committing, and the group reconvenes later. That process takes longer and may reach the same decision, but it is deliberation, not groupthink.'
    - id: chair-recommendation
      label: The chair opened with a recommendation.
      explanation: "Opening with a position is not inherently a problem. It becomes one when it prevents challenge and critical discussion. By itself, a recommendation is not groupthink."
  correct_option_ids:
    - private-concerns

Read the scenario and identify which behavior is the clearest signal of groupthink.
