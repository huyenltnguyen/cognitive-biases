A related pattern is that we evaluate the same behavior more favorably when it comes from someone we consider part of our group than from someone outside it. Our team, our department, the people we have worked with for a long time all receive a more generous reading than newcomers or outsiders do.

This shows up in specific ways. When a well-regarded colleague produces an ambiguous result, we tend to interpret it as them doing their best in a difficult situation. When a newer or less-liked colleague produces the same result, we tend to interpret it as falling short. In hiring, candidates who share our background, references, or way of speaking feel safer and more qualified, even when those similarities have nothing to do with competence. In meetings, a point made by a senior or well-connected person receives attention, while the same point made earlier by someone more junior gets passed over.

In-group bias doesn't show up when someone clearly did well or clearly messed up. It shows up when the situation could be read either way. In those cases, who the person is to you determines how you read it.
