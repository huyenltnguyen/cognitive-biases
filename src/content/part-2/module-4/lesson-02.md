When a group appears to have reached a position, the social cost of disagreeing increases. This is partly an information effect — if the group seems confident, maybe they know something you don't. But it is also a social effect: disagreement is uncomfortable, and silence is easy.

Research suggests this can happen even when the correct answer is clear and observable. In Solomon Asch's line-judgment experiments (1950s), participants agreed with an obvious factual error made by a group — they could see the right answer, but the group's apparent confidence made dissent feel wrong. The degree of conformity varied across replications and contexts, but the basic pattern has been observed reliably.

In everyday group decisions: the first person to state a position has more influence than they realize, because every speaker afterward adjusts relative to that starting point. People who disagree privately often say nothing, assuming others probably know something they don't. The meeting record reflects a consensus that wasn't fully genuine.
