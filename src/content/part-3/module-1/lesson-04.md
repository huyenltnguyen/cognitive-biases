Inside-view thinking means reasoning from the specifics of the current situation: "Our team is experienced. Our plan is detailed. Our circumstances are different." This always feels reasonable because the details are real. But it consistently produces overoptimistic estimates for timelines, costs, and outcomes.

The outside view is the correction: before reasoning from the inside, ask what usually happens in situations like this one. Use that base rate as your starting point. Then adjust for what genuinely makes this situation different — conservatively, because that adjustment almost always feels larger than it actually is.

Three steps:

1. **Identify a reference class** — a set of similar situations with known outcomes.
2. **Find the distribution of outcomes** — how often they succeeded, how long they took, what they cost.
3. **Place your situation in that distribution** as the starting point, then make a considered adjustment for specific differences.

The most common error is choosing a reference class that is too narrow ("our last three projects") or too broad ("all projects of this type"). A useful reference class is large enough to reflect real statistical patterns and specific enough to be genuinely comparable.
