When a group is making a decision, people tend to adjust what they say toward what the room appears to believe. The first person to speak sets an anchor, and others drift toward it. The result can be a decision that reflects who spoke first or who spoke loudest, not what the individuals actually think.

In _Noise_ (2021), Daniel Kahneman, Olivier Sibony, and Cass Sunstein call this a core source of error in group judgment. Their recommended fix is straightforward: have each person form their judgment independently before the group discusses.

The technique is one sentence: before a discussion, write down what you think. One line is enough. Do it before you hear anyone else's view.

Once you have committed to a position, even privately on a piece of paper no one else will see, you are more resistant to pressure to abandon it. You might still change your mind, but the change comes from hearing something persuasive, not from wanting to avoid being the one who disagrees.

This also applies outside of meetings. If you are about to read comments on a proposal you reviewed, or check how others voted on something you have an opinion about, write your own view down first.
