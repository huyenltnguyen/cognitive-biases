exercise:
  - kind: multiple-choice
    mode: single
    question: 'A couple is about to sign a lease on an apartment in a new neighborhood. They visited twice on Saturday mornings and loved it — quiet streets, good cafés, friendly people. They also read positive reviews from current residents. Which action would best challenge their impression?'
    options:
      - id: compare-a
        label: 'Shortlist two other neighborhoods and visit those as well'
        explanation: "Comparing options is practical, but it doesn't test whether their impression of this neighborhood holds up under different conditions. They could visit alternatives on Saturday mornings too and learn nothing new."
      - id: visit-evening-a
        label: 'Visit at a different time — a weekday evening or late at night'
        explanation: "Correct. Their impression is based entirely on Saturday mornings. Visiting at different times directly exposes them to conditions they haven't seen — noise levels, parking, street activity — without relying on anyone else's opinion."
      - id: reviews-a
        label: 'Search for negative reviews from people who moved away'
        explanation: "Better than reading more positive reviews, but online complaints tend to focus on extreme cases. It's secondhand information when they could check the conditions themselves."
      - id: more-visits-a
        label: 'Visit a third time to see if their impression holds'
        explanation: "A third Saturday morning visit will feel like confirmation. The issue isn't how many times they've visited — it's that they've only seen the neighborhood under one set of conditions."
    correct_option_ids:
      - visit-evening-a

  - kind: multiple-choice
    mode: single
    question: "A parent is convinced that her son's poor grades in one class are the teacher's fault. He does well in every other subject, and two other parents have the same complaint. She's about to request a meeting with the head of department. Which action would best challenge her thinking?"
    options:
      - id: talk-teacher-b
        label: 'Talk to the teacher before going to the department head'
        explanation: "A reasonable step, but she could go into that conversation still assuming the teacher is the problem. Talking to the person you've already decided is at fault isn't the same as looking for a different explanation."
      - id: more-parents-b
        label: "Ask more parents whether they've had similar problems"
        explanation: "More parents who agree will feel like stronger evidence, but she's only asking people who share her complaint. This adds volume to her existing view, not a different perspective."
      - id: scores-b
        label: 'Look at his test scores and assignments in detail'
        explanation: "This might confirm the grades are bad, but it doesn't challenge her explanation for why. She already knows the grades are poor — the question is whether the teacher is actually the reason."
      - id: study-habits-b
        label: 'Ask her son how he studies for this class compared to his other classes'
        explanation: "Correct. This is the explanation she hasn't looked into. If he studies differently for this subject — or doesn't study for it at all — that changes the picture. It directly tests her assumption rather than building a bigger case for it."
    correct_option_ids:
      - study-habits-b

  - kind: multiple-choice
    mode: single
    question: "A community group wants the council to close a residential street to through-traffic. They've collected dozens of complaints from residents about noise and speeding, and presented photos of cars driving dangerously. Which action would best challenge their proposal?"
    options:
      - id: accident-stats-c
        label: 'Check official accident statistics for the street'
        explanation: 'Useful data, but this still focuses on whether the street is dangerous — the thing they already believe. Even if the stats are low, they can argue the danger is underreported.'
      - id: petition-c
        label: 'Collect signatures from a wider area, not just this street'
        explanation: "A broader petition might show support, but it doesn't test whether the closure would actually solve the problem or create new ones."
      - id: traffic-study-c
        label: 'Ask the council to do a traffic study on neighboring streets before deciding'
        explanation: 'Correct. If this street closes, the traffic has to go somewhere. Studying the neighboring streets forces the group to look at what their proposal would actually do — not just what it would fix.'
      - id: meeting-c
        label: 'Present the complaints at a public council meeting'
        explanation: "This is an advocacy step — it makes the case louder, but doesn't challenge whether the proposed solution has been thought through."
    correct_option_ids:
      - traffic-study-c
---

There are three scenarios. For each, pick the action that would best challenge the decision-maker's current thinking.
