When you're leaning toward a conclusion, your brain filters incoming evidence to match. You don't notice it happening. And simply wanting to be more objective doesn't help much — the filtering is automatic.

One reliable way to interrupt it: before you finalize a decision, ask yourself, "What would have to be true for the opposite conclusion to be correct?" Then go looking for evidence of that. This is sometimes called "consider the opposite" or steelmanning.

You don't have to change your mind. The point is to seriously engage with the strongest version of the case against your position. Sometimes that case turns out to be weak, and your original conclusion is better for having survived the test. Sometimes it turns out to be strong, and you've caught something important before it was too late.

You can do this alone in five minutes, or build it into a group meeting as a step before any final decision.
